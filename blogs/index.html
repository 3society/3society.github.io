The Universal Property of The Cone $(X\times Y, p_1, p_2)$

Given two vector spaces $X$ and $Y$, the cone $$(X\times Y, p_1\colon X\times Y \to X, p_2\colon X\times Y \to Y)$$ where $p_1$ and $p_2$ are natural projections has the universal property that for any cone $(V,f_1\colon V \to X,f_2\colon V \to Y)$ there exists a unique linear map $f \colon V \to X\times Y$ such that $p_1f = f_1$ and $p_2f = f_2$.

The linear map $f$ sends a vector $v$ to pairs $(f_1(v), f_2(v))$. Suppose there was another map $f'$ that did the same. Then, since $(p_1, p_2) = 1_{X \times Y}$, it follows that $$f' = (p_1,p_2)f' = (p_1f', p_2f') = (f_1, f_2) = f.$$

To show uniqueness of the cone with this property, suppose there was another cone $(P', p_1', p_2')$ with the same property. Then, there is a unique linear map $f \colon P' \to X\times Y$ such that $p_1f = p_1'$ and $p_2f = p_2'$. Similarly, we get a unique linear map $f' \colon X\times Y \to P'$ with $p_1'f' = p_1$ and $p_2'f' = p_2$. Now we can compose the two maps to get $f'f\colon P' \to P'$. Since, for the cone $(P', p_1', p_2')$, we have that $$p_1'f'f = p_1f = p_1' = p_1'1_{P'}$$ and $$p_2'f'f = p_2f = p_2' = p_2'1_{P'},$$ uniqueness follows from universality, leading to $f'f = 1_{P'}$. Following similar steps, we can show $ff' = 1_{X\times Y}$. Hence $f$ is an isomorphism.

The Universal Property of $\mathbb Z[x]$

The ring $\mathbb Z[x]$ is unique ring satisfying the universal property that for any ring $R$ and any $r \in R$ there exists a unique ring homomorphism $\phi \colon \mathbb Z[x] \to R$ such that $\phi(x) = r$.

Let $A$ be a ring and $a \in A$, where for all rings $R$ and all $r \in R$, there exists a unique ring homomorphism $\phi \colon A \to R$ such that $\phi(a) = r$. We know that $\mathbb Z [x]$ has this property.

Take $R = \mathbb Z [x]$ and $r = x \in \mathbb Z[x]$. Then we have a unique homomorphism $\psi \colon A \to \mathbb Z[x]$. Now, as $\mathbb Z[x]$ has the same property, we get a unique homomorphism $\psi'\colon \mathbb Z[x] \to A$ with $r = a$. Since $\psi' \psi \colon A \to A$ sends $\psi' \psi (a) = \psi'(x) = a$ the composition is unique by the universal property. Hence, $1_A = \psi'\psi$ ($\implies \psi$ is injective and $\psi'$ is surjective). Similarly, $\psi \psi' \colon \mathbb Z[x] \to \mathbb Z[x]$ is unique, so $1_{\mathbb Z[x]} = \psi \psi'$. This shows that the homomorphisms are mutually inverse, and therefore isomorphisms, $\mathbb Z[x] \cong A$.

Pushouts

A pushout of two morphisms $f\colon Z \to X$ and $g\colon Z \to Y$ is a triple $(P, i\colon X \to P, j\colon Y \to P)$ such that $if = jg$ and such that for any other triple $(Q, i'\colon X \to Q, j'\colon Y \to Q)$ such that $i'f = j'g$, there must exist a unique $b \colon P \to Q$ such that $ui = i'$ and $uj = j'$.

Tensor Products

Let $U$ and $V$ be vector spaces. A tensor product is a vector space denoted $U\otimes V$ with an associated bilinear map $b$ mapping pairs $(u,v) \in U \times V$ to an element in $U\otimes V$ denoted $u \otimes v$, such that for any vector space $W$ and for any bilinear map $f \colon U \times V \to W$, there exists a unique linear map $\bar{f} \colon U\otimes V \to W$ such that $f = \bar{f}b$, and the pair $(U\otimes V, b)$ is unique (up to isomorphism) satisfying this (universal) property.

From this we see that the bilinear map out of $U \times V$ is the same thing as the linear map out of $U \otimes V$, which reduces the study of bilinear maps to the study of linear maps.

$\mathbb{R}[x]/(x^2+1)\cong\mathbb{C}$

Let $f \colon \mathbb{R}[x] \to \mathbb{C}$ where $f(x) \mapsto f(i)$. This is clearly onto. Since $x^2+1$ is a minimal polynomial and $\mathbb{R}[x]$ is a PID the ideal generated by $x^2+1$ is maximal. Since $x^2+1 \in \ker f$, it follows that $\ker f = (x^2+1)$, hence by the first isomorphism theorem the claim follows.
